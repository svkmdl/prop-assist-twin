# Real Estate Acquisition Guide: XYZ Immobilienverwaltung GmbH

Buying a property in Germany is a structured legal process. At **XYZ Immobilienverwaltung GmbH**, we guide our clients through every step—from the initial search to the final handover. This document outlines the standard procedure for purchasing residential or commercial property through our agency.

---

## Phase 1: Preparation and Financial Framework

Before viewing properties, it is essential to have your financial "house in order."

* **Financing Certificate (*Finanzierungsbestätigung*):** In the current German market, sellers rarely consider offers without a preliminary confirmation from a bank. We recommend securing this before your first viewing.
* **Budgeting for Ancillary Costs (*Kaufnebenkosten*):** Remember that the purchase price is not the final cost. In Germany, you must budget an additional **10% to 12%** for:
    * **Property Transfer Tax (*Grunderwerbsteuer*):** Varies by federal state (e.g., 6% in Berlin/Hesse, 3.5% in Bavaria).
    * **Notary and Land Registry Fees:** Approximately 1.5% to 2%.
    * **Brokerage Commission (*Maklercourtage*):** Usually 3.57% (including VAT) following the legal split between buyer and seller.

## Phase 2: Property Search and Viewing

* **Individual Appointments:** Unlike many agencies, XYZ Immobilienverwaltung GmbH avoids mass viewings. We schedule individual slots to allow for a detailed technical inspection.
* **Document Review:** Once you find a property of interest, we provide a full digital "Data Room" containing:

    * The Energy Certificate (*Energieausweis*).
    * Floor plans and cubic volume calculations.
    * (For Apartments) Minutes of the last three owners' meetings and the Declaration of Division (*Teilungserklärung*).

## Phase 3: The Offer and Reservation

* **Purchase Offer:** Submit your formal offer in writing. This should include your proposed price, intended move-in date, and your financing confirmation.
* **Reservation Agreement:** If the seller accepts, we can draft a reservation agreement. This typically involves a small fee (usually deductible from the final commission) to take the property off the market while the notary prepares the contract.

---

## Phase 4: The Notary and the Purchase Contract

In Germany, all real estate transactions must be notarized.

1.  **Drafting:** The notary (usually chosen by the buyer) prepares a draft of the *Kaufvertrag* (Purchase Contract).
2.  **Review Period:** By law, you have 14 days to review the draft if you are a private individual buying from a professional seller.
3.  **The Signing (*Beurkundung*):** Both parties meet at the notary’s office. The notary reads the entire contract aloud to ensure total transparency.
4.  **Priority Notice (*Vormerkung*):** After signing, the notary secures the property for you in the Land Register so the seller cannot sell it to anyone else.

---

## Phase 5: Payment and Handover

* **Payment Notification (*Fälligkeitsmitteilung*):** Once the notary has secured all necessary permits and the priority notice is filed, you will receive a notification to pay the purchase price. This usually happens 4–8 weeks after the signing.
* **Tax Payment:** You will pay the Property Transfer Tax to the *Finanzamt*, after which they issue a "Clearance Certificate" (*Unbedenklichkeitsbescheinigung*), allowing the final transfer of title.
* **The Handover (*Übergabe*):** Once the seller confirms receipt of the full purchase price, the keys are handed over. A protocol is signed to document meter readings (water, heating, electricity) and the condition of the property.
